Undergraduate Thesis


The global push for energy conservation and energy-efficient systems has fueled the interest in heat exchanger research. Furthermore, the effect of the quality of the grid and mesh generation software on the accuracy, convergence, and computational resource has not been demonstrated in the literature. The research in heat exchanger enhancement through improving the design and further contribute to current literature was the basis for this research.
This thesis presents the optimization of an existing compact heat exchanger design using data from computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analyses. The geometry chosen for the study was a commonly found Z configuration heat exchanger with two branches.

The geometry was then discretized into a variety of grids and quantitative comparison of generated meshes and the effects of meshing parameters provided a suitable approach to mesh the geometry.

With the boundary conditions and domain, the fluid field was solved using CFD simulations through ANSYS CFX 19.2. A parametric study was conducted to identify the factors that produce an efficient and optimal design of a branching flow heat exchanger.

Mesh Analysis

The quantitative comparison of generated meshes and the effects of meshing parameters through the mesh analyses is a key aspect investigated in this study. Using ANSYS meshing, three tetrahedral and three hexahedral grids were produced with varying levels of refinement. Using ICEM meshing, three levels of refinement were produced for a hexahedral mesh.
The independence of the grids was evaluated on two levels, local and global. Local velocity profiles at two locations were used to judge the grids ability to provide convergent results. The flow at sample line 1 is not too complex to ensure the grids can accurately solve the general flow while sample line 2 was used to study how the grid resolves more complex flow with recirculation. Hexahedral reference grid ~4 million nodes. The global parameters used to evaluate convergence were the non-dimensional pressure drop and non-dimensional mass flow rate distribution between the branches. Furthermore, the order of convergence and grid convergence index (GCI) were determined for each grid set.

Parametric Study

In the parametric study, the Reynolds number, number of branches and spacing was varied with the inlet diameter held constant. Furthermore, the width of the branches (10mm) and the length of the inlet and outlet portion (5D) are kept constant during each case. The parametric study was conducted to determine the effects of the parameters on the non-dimensional pressure drop and the misdistribution of flow.

In conclusion, the CFD analysis provided a valuable tool to optimize the design of a branching flow heat exchanger. The mesh analysis determined the optimal meshing approach that results in a reliable, convergent, accurate, and efficient simulation for future studies in the growing field on heat exchanger research.
